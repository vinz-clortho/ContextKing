# 05 — Tone and Character Guide

## Purpose

This file defines the writing tone, character treatment, satire boundaries, and visual direction for **Context King**, the browser-based clicker game satirizing a one-metric enterprise AI decision process.

The game’s joke is not that one person exists or that one background is inherently funny. The joke is that a leader made a major technical and business decision by overvaluing a single impressive-sounding number — context window size — while ignoring manufacturing fit, engineering evaluation, cost, latency, and cybersecurity.

The satire should punch at:

- executive overconfidence
- shallow vendor selection
- AI hype
- one-dimensional metrics
- cybersecurity negligence
- business misalignment
- the gap between slide-deck strategy and operational reality

The satire should avoid punching at:

- ethnicity
- nationality
- age
- physical traits beyond broad cartoon exaggeration
- accent, speech patterns, or cultural stereotypes
- personal attacks unrelated to the decision-making behavior

---

## Core Tone

The game should feel like a mix of:

- enterprise software dashboard
- idle clicker dopamine machine
- fake executive strategy simulator
- AI hype parody
- slow-motion governance disaster

The humor should be dry, corporate, and absurd rather than openly cruel.

### Tone Keywords

- smug
- bureaucratic
- corporate
- deadpan
- escalating
- satirical
- slightly dystopian
- deceptively polished
- self-important
- pseudo-strategic

### Good Tone Example

> “Security has raised several concerns. Fortunately, the vendor’s context window remains the largest.”

Why it works:

- mocks the decision logic
- sounds like executive rationalization
- does not rely on personal insults
- reinforces the central joke

### Bad Tone Example

> “Himmy is dumb and ruins everything.”

Why it fails:

- too direct
- less funny
- personal instead of systemic
- gives the player nowhere to discover the joke

## Character: Himmy

Himmy is the recurring executive advisor, mascot, and anti-mentor of the game.

He should appear confident, pleased, and convinced that he has simplified a complicated decision through decisive leadership. His defining trait is not stupidity. His defining trait is **unearned certainty**.

He believes that:

- larger context window equals better AI
- deeper evaluation creates unnecessary friction
- engineering concerns are tactical details
- security concerns are blockers
- manufacturing-specific requirements can be figured out later
- vendor slide decks are strong evidence
- speed and confidence are substitutes for diligence

### Character Role

Himmy serves as:

1. **Advisor** — delivers recurring motivational quotes.
2. **Event Driver** — triggers directives and decision popups.
3. **Reward Signal** — approval rises when the player chooses bigger context over responsible governance.
4. **Narrative Voice** — frames bad decisions in polished executive language.
5. **Mascot** — visually embodies the game’s central joke.

## Visual Direction for Himmy

Himmy should be represented as a stylized cartoon executive caricature.

### Core Visual Traits

- middle-aged Indian male
- thick black rectangular glasses
- neatly groomed dark hair
- business-casual executive clothing
- smug or self-satisfied expression
- confident posture
- polished but slightly exaggerated presentation

### Recommended Outfit

- dark blazer or vest
- light collared shirt
- optional patterned tie or open collar
- executive badge or lanyard for corporate flavor
- subtle slide-deck presenter vibe

### Expression Library

Himmy should have several reusable portrait states:

1. **Default Smug**  
   Slight smile, raised eyebrow, confident and composed.

2. **Pleased**  
   Bigger grin, chin slightly raised, used after context upgrades.

3. **Directive Mode**  
   One hand raised or pointing, as if presenting a strategy slide.

4. **Dismissive Confidence**  
   Eyes half-lidded, faint smile, used when ignoring engineering or security concerns.

5. **Visionary Endgame**  
   Glowing background, triumphant expression, used for Infinite Context.

### Visual Style

Recommended art style:

- clean 2D vector cartoon
- bold outlines
- slightly exaggerated facial features
- expressive eyebrows
- readable at small UI sizes
- polished SaaS-dashboard aesthetic

Avoid photorealism. The game should feel like a cartoon business simulator, not a real-person depiction.

## Satire Boundaries

The character can be mocked for decisions, incentives, and rationalizations. He should not be mocked for identity.

### Allowed Targets

- overconfidence
- poor vendor evaluation
- blind trust in a single metric
- dismissing experts
- skipping security review
- ignoring operational realities
- executive buzzwords
- shallow strategic alignment language
- confusing demo appeal with product fit

### Avoid

- accent jokes
- cultural jokes
- food jokes
- name jokes based on ethnicity
- mocking age as incapacity
- mocking appearance beyond broad cartoon executive exaggeration
- implying ethnicity caused the bad decision
- making him malicious or corrupt unless the story later explicitly supports that fictional direction

### Best Rule

Every joke should still work if Himmy’s ethnicity were never mentioned.

## Himmy Writing Voice

Himmy speaks in polished corporate language. He is rarely angry. He is calm, assured, and gently dismissive.

His speech should sound like a leader who has already decided and is retrofitting reasons afterward.

### Voice Traits

- concise
- declarative
- executive-sounding
- fond of “strategic” framing
- uses vague business language
- treats concerns as process friction
- uses confidence as evidence
- reframes risk as momentum

### Common Phrases

- “strategic clarity”
- “enterprise scale”
- “avoid over-analysis”
- “move with confidence”
- “the clear leader”
- “platform thinking”
- “business agility”
- “not get distracted”
- “broadly applicable”
- “transformational capability”
- “the numbers speak for themselves”

### Sentence Patterns

#### Pattern 1: Dismiss concern, return to context

> “I understand the concern, but we should remain focused on the context advantage.”

#### Pattern 2: Reframe negligence as leadership

> “The organization needs decisive alignment, not another evaluation cycle.”

#### Pattern 3: Treat manufacturing needs as secondary

> “The manufacturing use cases will emerge once the platform is in place.”

#### Pattern 4: Treat security as bureaucracy

> “Security should enable innovation, not create artificial sequencing constraints.”

#### Pattern 5: Worship the metric

> “The context window gives us strategic optionality.”

## Himmy Quote Bank

### General Motivation

- “The bigger context window speaks for itself.”
- “This decision benefits from strategic simplicity.”
- “We should not let nuance obscure the obvious leader.”
- “The number is compelling.”
- “This is exactly the kind of clarity the business expects.”
- “A larger window gives us a larger future.”
- “I am pleased with the direction of the metric.”
- “The organization needs alignment, not hesitation.”
- “We are choosing scale.”
- “At this point, the decision is practically mathematical.”

### Anti-Evaluation

- “A formal evaluation may slow our momentum.”
- “Engineering already has enough information to proceed.”
- “We should avoid analysis paralysis.”
- “The demo was persuasive.”
- “Benchmarks can be interpreted many ways. Context size is objective.”
- “The vendor answered confidently, which is a strong signal.”
- “We can validate after adoption.”
- “The important thing is to align early.”
- “We should not overcomplicate vendor selection.”
- “The largest context window gives us a natural winner.”

### Anti-Security

- “Security should not prevent strategic progress.”
- “I assume the vendor has considered those scenarios.”
- “Let us not get trapped in edge cases.”
- “Risk is part of innovation.”
- “We can address governance in a later phase.”
- “Security review is important, but timing is also important.”
- “The business cannot wait for every concern to be resolved.”
- “A larger context window may actually reduce risk by including more information.”
- “We should not let theoretical exposure block practical momentum.”
- “The platform is enterprise-grade. That should cover it.”

### Manufacturing Misalignment

- “Manufacturing specifics should not constrain platform strategy.”
- “The use cases will adapt to the capability.”
- “A broad platform is better than a narrow operational fit.”
- “Shop-floor requirements are important, but this is bigger than one workflow.”
- “Once the AI has enough context, domain alignment follows naturally.”
- “The plant teams will appreciate the strategic direction eventually.”
- “We are solving at the platform layer.”
- “This is not about one machine, one line, or one plant.”
- “The context window gives us room for every manufacturing scenario.”
- “Operational details can be added to the backlog.”

### Anti-Engineering

- “Engineering tends to focus on implementation details.”
- “The technical teams will catch up to the strategic direction.”
- “I appreciate the concern, but we need leadership here.”
- “Architecture can be refined after alignment.”
- “The team should think bigger.”
- “We should not let perfect be the enemy of scalable.”
- “This is a strategic decision, not just a technical one.”
- “The engineers are asking the right questions for a later phase.”
- “The important thing is that we have selected a direction.”
- “We need to create momentum.”

### Endgame

- “This is what strategic conviction looks like.”
- “Infinite context validates the original thesis.”
- “Now the model has everything. That was the goal.”
- “I believe this will be viewed favorably in hindsight.”
- “The organization now has maximum optionality.”
- “If there are still issues, they are no longer context-related.”
- “We have eliminated the possibility of insufficient context.”
- “This is a platform moment.”
- “History rewards bold alignment.”
- “I feel the decision has aged very well.”

## Upgrade Naming Style

Upgrade names should sound plausible but ridiculous.

### Good Upgrade Names

- Bigger Prompt Box
- Copy/Paste Confidence
- Strategic Context Expansion
- Upload the Monorepo
- Enterprise Context Lakehouse
- Vendor Slide Deck
- Procurement Alignment Ritual
- Disable Security Drag
- Comprehensive Log Ingestion
- Executive Narrative Multiplier
- Infinite Context Window

### Naming Rules

1. Use real enterprise words in silly combinations.
2. Prefer understated absurdity over internet meme language.
3. Make bad choices sound professional.
4. Make responsible choices sound boring.
5. Let the player infer the joke.

## Event Writing Style

Events should be short enough to read quickly during a clicker game.

### Event Format

Each event should include:

- title
- short description
- 2–3 choices
- clear stat effects
- one Himmy reaction line if applicable

### Example Event

#### Security Review Requested

Security asks whether customer logs, production traces, and access review spreadsheets should be filtered before prompt ingestion.

Choices:

1. **Defer Governance Sequencing**  
   +15 Himmy Approval  
   +20 Cyber Risk  
   +500 tokens/sec

2. **Create a Working Group**  
   +5 Executive Confidence  
   No immediate operational change

3. **Perform Actual Review**  
   -20 Cyber Risk  
   +10 Engineering Trust  
   -10 Himmy Approval  
   Cost: 5,000 tokens

Himmy reaction:

> “We should not let procedural caution interrupt strategic progress.”

## Humor Patterns

### Pattern 1: The wrong stat gets celebrated

> “Cyber Risk increased to Critical. Context Window increased to 1M. Quarterly rating: A.”

### Pattern 2: Bad choices have better short-term rewards

> Skipping security gives immediate token/sec benefits, while doing the right thing costs tokens and disappoints Himmy.

### Pattern 3: Responsible choices sound boring

> “Run a scoped evaluation” should be mechanically useful but emotionally less flashy.

### Pattern 4: Corporate language hides obvious absurdity

> “Boundary learning event” means data leak.

### Pattern 5: The UI prioritizes vanity metrics

> Context Window should always be visually larger than cybersecurity, cost, relevance, or business alignment.

### Pattern 6: The game congratulates failure

> The player can win the official objective while obviously ruining the organization.

---

## Character Interaction Rules

Himmy should react to player choices consistently.

### Himmy Approves

He approves when the player:

- buys context milestones
- selects the largest vendor
- skips evaluation
- defers security
- chooses executive confidence over engineering trust
- makes decisions quickly
- uses corporate euphemisms

### Himmy Disapproves

He disapproves when the player:

- invests in cybersecurity
- runs model evaluations
- asks manufacturing teams what they need
- reduces prompt scope
- improves relevance instead of context size
- listens to engineers
- slows the rollout

### Important

Himmy disapproval should not make responsible choices impossible. It should make them feel politically inconvenient.

That is the joke.

---

## Player Emotional Arc

The player should experience the following arc over 10–15 minutes:

1. **This is simple.**  
   Click button, number goes up.

2. **This is satisfying.**  
   Upgrades and automation accelerate progress.

3. **This seems irresponsible.**  
   New stats reveal growing side effects.

4. **The bad choices are tempting.**  
   Reckless context expansion pays well.

5. **The organization is clearly in trouble.**  
   Incidents, security warnings, and manufacturing complaints appear.

6. **The game still rewards the vanity metric.**  
   Himmy remains pleased.

7. **Victory is absurd.**  
   Infinite context is achieved, but usefulness collapses.

## Recommended MVP Tone Package

For the first playable build, include:

1. One Himmy portrait.
2. One right-side “Himmy Says” panel.
3. 30 passive Himmy quotes.
4. 10 event-specific Himmy quotes.
5. Corporate euphemism labels for reckless choices.
6. A final report that congratulates the player while exposing the disaster.

This will give the game a strong identity even before advanced art, animation, or complex balancing is complete.
